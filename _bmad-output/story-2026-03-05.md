## Story Information

**Story Type:** Origin Story (Unfinished)

**Framework Used:** Origin Story — an origin story that hasn't ended yet. Two people in act one, asking a mentor figure whether there's an act two. Combined with elements of Pitch Narrative for the proof-of-concept beat and the structured ask at the end.

**Purpose:** 30-minute presentation to a Cambridge Professor of AI seeking advice and validation on business direction. Two non-tech founders (ROG — our tool CEO; Paul Speight — Director at Lloyds Banking Group, workforce optimization for 20,000 colleagues, AI-driven solutions) want to build an AI-powered tools business creating value for enterprise customers. Connection to Chris Gowland (CCO, Nuffield Health; ex-Lloyds digital transformation leader) provides both credibility and a live use case.

**Target Audience:** Dr. Raoul-Gabriel Urma — Founder & Group CEO of Cambridge Spark. PhD Computer Science (Cambridge), First-Class (Imperial), Google/eBay/Oracle/Goldman Sachs, Harvard Business School OPM. Founded Cambridge Spark in 2016 — specialist AI education provider that has trained 20,000+ professionals and generated £300M+ in client business value. Clients include Lloyds Banking Group (Paul Speight's employer). Lectures at University of Cambridge. YPO AI/ML Chair. His entire business IS the adoption gap thesis — he built a company around helping organisations build internal AI capabilities. He doesn't need convincing that "boring tools" matter. He needs to see you're serious builders with real output.

**Key Messages:**
- We are enthusiastic builders, not pretending to be AI researchers — we bring enterprise operations and commercial acumen
- The opportunity is in the gap between AI capability and AI adoption (the dot visualisation — 84% of humanity hasn't touched AI)
- We have a working proof of concept: our tool for Nuffield Health — AI-powered consultant profile quality intelligence
- We want your guidance: you've scaled an AI business from zero — help us see what we're missing
- Stephen Bartlett framing: "We are so, so, so early" — the market is wide open for applied AI tools that solve real business problems
- "Boring tools" thesis: the real AI revolution isn't giant models, it's small focused tools that remove friction — like electricity ending up inside washing machines, not power stations

**Assets:**
- Dot visualisation (each dot = 3.2M people, 84% grey/never used AI)
- Stephen Bartlett quote on AI adoption
- "Boring tools" essay / electricity analogy
- Live our tool/Nuffield Health screenshots (dashboard, AI assessments, profile rewrites, copilot)
- Paul Speight's enterprise operations background (workforce planning at scale — credibility, NOT a current Lloyds engagement)
- Chris Gowland connection (CCO Nuffield Health)

**Critical framing note:** This is NOT a company pitch. This is two people with good careers exploring a side gig. The real question to Raoul is: "Do you see a genuine market need? Is there a scale opportunity?" The answer determines whether they go all in — Paul would leave Lloyds. They're at the "should we jump?" stage, not the "we've jumped" stage. No company names, no branding. Raw and honest.

**Constraints:**
- 30 minutes total — big words, minimal detail
- Honest tone — self-aware, not overselling
- Must land as credible to someone who knows AI deeply

## Story Structure

### Opening Hook

*[Full-screen dot visualisation. No title slide. No logos. Just the image and silence.]*

"Each dot is 3.2 million people. Look at all that grey."

*[Three-second pause.]*

"That's 6.8 billion people who have never used AI. Ever. In any form. And I spend too much time on Twitter convincing myself the whole world has lapped me."

The hook works because it's visual, immediate, and counter-intuitive. Everyone in an AI meeting assumes AI is everywhere. The grey sea says otherwise. The self-deprecating admission ("I spend too much time on Twitter") humanises the presenter instantly.

### Core Narrative

Two enterprise operators — not tech founders, not AI researchers — who keep noticing the same thing: the gap between what AI can do and what organisations actually do with it is staggering. They've stopped waiting for someone else to close the gap and started building tools themselves. They've produced a working proof of concept. Now they're sitting in front of someone who's already scaled an AI business and asking the honest question: should we jump?

### Key Story Beats

**Beat 1 — The Spark:** The dot visualisation. 2,500 dots, 84% grey. "I showed a simple AI tool to a mate from my hometown and they reacted like I'd shown them fire." Most people have no idea what's happening. Stephen Bartlett: "We are so, so, so early."

**Beat 2 — Who We Are:** Two enterprise operators. Paul — workforce planning for 20,000 people, knows where operational friction lives. ROG — builder, enthusiast, hands-on with AI tools daily. Neither is a computer scientist. Both keep seeing the same gap.

**Beat 3 — The Boring Tools Thesis:** The flashy AI phase is fading. Like electricity in the 1900s — the headlines were about power stations, but civilisation changed because of washing machines and desk lamps. The real AI revolution is small, focused tools that solve one specific task repeatedly. And the barrier to building them has collapsed.

**Beat 4 — We Built One:** "We didn't just talk about it. We built a tool." One or two sentences on what it does — AI analyses 3,800 consultant profiles for a healthcare provider, scores quality, identifies gaps, suggests improvements. Screenshots rotating behind you while you talk — dashboard, AI assessments, rewrites — but you don't stop to demo. The point is: it exists, it works, we'll send you the full thing afterwards. Move on fast.

**Beat 5 — The Question:** We think this pattern is repeatable. Domain → friction → small AI tool → value. But before we bet our careers on it, we want your honest read. Is the market real? Can it scale? What are we not seeing?

### Emotional Arc

**Opening (0-5 min) — Curiosity + Humility**
Start with the dot picture. Let the silence do the work. All that grey. The audience feels the scale of the gap. Then the personal touch — "I showed a mate a simple AI tool and he reacted like I'd shown him fire." Self-deprecating. Honest. Sets the tone: we're not here to impress you, we're here to share something we noticed.

**Rising (5-13 min) — Recognition + Energy**
"Who we are" grounds credibility without overselling. Then the boring tools thesis builds energy — the electricity analogy clicks instantly. The audience shifts from "who are these guys" to "they're seeing something real." The reframe from flashy AI to boring tools is intellectually satisfying — especially for someone like Raoul who lives this daily.

**Proof Point (13-15 min) — Confidence + Restraint**
"We built one." Screenshots flash. You don't linger. The restraint is the point — you're not desperately demo-ing. You're saying: it exists, it works, judge it on your own time. This signals confidence.

**The Turn (15-17 min) — Vulnerability + Stakes**
"We think this is repeatable. We think there's a business here. But we're not naive — we have good careers and we're not going to throw them away on a feeling. So we're asking you: is this real?" This is the emotional peak. The honesty disarms. Raoul has been in this exact position.

**Discussion (17-30 min) — Open + Engaged**
The energy shifts to dialogue. You're listening more than talking. Raoul is the expert now. The emotional note is mutual respect — you've earned the conversation by being honest about where you are.

### Resolution/Call to Action

The resolution is not a sales close. It's an honest ask:

"We'd value three things from you:
1. **Your honest read** — do you see a real market need for this kind of applied AI tooling?
2. **The scale question** — if the pattern works, is there a path to something bigger?
3. **What we're missing** — what would you tell two people at this stage that you wish someone had told you?"

Then: "We'll send you the full demo and the tool afterwards. Take a proper look. And if you think there's something here, we'd love to keep the conversation going."

## Story Elements Analysis

### Character/Voice

Two honest, slightly self-deprecating enterprise operators. Not pretending to be tech founders or AI researchers. The strength is in the self-awareness: "We know what we don't know." Conversational, direct, no jargon. The kind of people you'd trust with a straight answer because they're giving you one.

### Conflict/Tension

**Internal tension:** Do we jump? We have good careers. This feels real but feelings aren't a business case. The "side gig" framing is honest — we haven't burned our boats yet.

**External tension:** "We are so, so, so early" cuts both ways. The window is open now. 82% of businesses aren't using AI. But that window won't stay open forever. If we wait for certainty, we'll miss it.

**Audience tension:** Will Raoul validate or challenge the direction? Both outcomes are valuable — that's the honesty of the ask.

### Transformation/Change

Hasn't happened yet. That's the point. You're in act one. Asking whether there's an act two. The transformation is the decision that comes *after* this meeting.

### Emotional Touchpoints

- The dot picture — visceral, visual, immediate ("look at all that grey")
- "Showing fire to a mate" — relatable, human, funny
- The boring tools / electricity analogy — reframes AI from scary to useful
- The working prototype — proof you're not just talking
- "Should we jump?" — vulnerability, honesty, stakes

### Key Messages

1. The AI adoption gap is massive and most people are looking at the wrong end of it
2. Small, boring, focused AI tools are where the real value lives
3. You don't need to be a tech expert to build them — you need to understand the workflow
4. We've actually built one and it works — here's the proof
5. We're not here to pitch — we're here to ask: is this real enough to bet on?

## Usage Guidelines

### Best Channels

- **Primary:** In-person 30-minute presentation with slides
- **Pre-meeting:** Short email setting context (use short version above)
- **Post-meeting:** Follow-up email with demo link and recap (medium version)
- **Supporting material:** The working Nuffield tool itself — send as proof after the meeting

### Audience Considerations

Raoul is a founder-CEO who has:
- Heard hundreds of AI pitches — don't sound like one
- Built and scaled his own AI business — respect his experience, don't explain his own market to him
- Trained 20,000 professionals — he knows the adoption gap intimately
- Worked with Lloyds Banking Group — he may already know Paul's world
- Done Cambridge PhD + Harvard OPM — intellectually rigorous, will spot fluff instantly

**What will land:** Honesty, self-awareness, a working prototype, smart questions
**What will fall flat:** Buzzwords, inflated claims, pretending to know more than you do, asking for things you haven't earned

### Tone AND Voice Notes

- **Conversational, not corporate** — talk like two people having a beer with someone they respect
- **Self-deprecating but not self-doubting** — "we're not tech experts" is honest; "we probably can't do this" is weak
- **Confident about what you've built, humble about what you don't know** — the prototype proves you can execute; the questions prove you're thoughtful
- **No jargon** — ironic given the audience, but Raoul will respect plain language more than buzzword bingo
- **Pacing** — slow on the dot picture (let it breathe), quick on credentials (don't dwell), measured on the thesis, fast on the demo (flash and move), open on the questions

### Adaptation Suggestions

- If Raoul engages early and starts asking questions during the thesis section, let the structure go — the conversation IS the presentation
- If he challenges the "boring tools" framing, that's a gift — it means he's engaging seriously. Don't defend, explore: "What are you seeing that we're missing?"
- If he's sceptical about non-technical builders, the prototype is your answer — don't argue the theory, point to the evidence
- Have Paul ready to speak to the enterprise pain points from lived experience — Raoul may want to hear from both of you, not just one presenter
